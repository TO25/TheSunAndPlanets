Uranus


Origin of the name

Uranus was named for the Roman god who was the father of the Titans. The name was suggested by German astronomer Johann Bode. In the way Saturn was Jupiter’s father, Uranus was Saturn’s father. So the three planets are son, father, and grandfather. Uranus’s many moons are named after characters in plays by William Shakespeare and poems of Alexander Pope. British astronomer William Herschel, who discovered the planet named it after himself. Herschel also offered to name the planet after then King of England George III, wanting to name it the Georgium Sidus. Hypercronius was suggested, meaning "above cronus" with cronus being the Greek name for Saturn, this name related to the planets position by Saturn. Minerva was also suggested, the name of the Roman goddess of wisdom.

Uranus is the third largest planet in the solar system. It is the seventh planet from the Sun. Uranus was unknown to ancient astronomers, even though it is just visible with the naked eye in perfectly clear and dark skies. It’s so dim and has such a slow orbit compared to the other known planets that it didn’t register as one. It just looks like a faint pinpoint of greenish or bluish light. It was the first planet that was discovered through the use of a telescope. It was discovered by musician William Herschel from his back garden in Bath, England, in 1781. When he first saw Uranus, he thought it was a comet.

Uranus is 14 times more massive than Earth. The planet is similar to Neptune but has a paler blue, almost featureless face. It is the coldest of all the planets and generates very little heat from within. The planet contains less hydrogen and helium than Jupiter and Saturn but has more rock and water. Uranus is the third largest planet by radius and the fourth largest by mass. It is about 3 billion kilometers from the Sun, which means that it receives 0.0025 percent of the sunlight that the Earth gets.

It orbits on its side perhaps because it was knocked over by a collision with another planet early in its history. This means that its poles get more sunlight than does its equator. While other planets have extreme tilts, none are so perfectly perpendicular to the plane of its orbit. The tilt has impacted just about everything about Uranus. Its extreme tilt gives it very long seasons. Each season on Uranus lasts more than 20 Earth years.

Uranus is a cold, windy world made of gases and liquid. There is no land. It has a core of silicate rock, encased in ices and topped with a gaseous layer. The core must be very small, since Uranus is the second least dense planet. The ice mantle surrounding the core is fluid, with volatiles like methane, ammonia and water.

The atmosphere contains three layers: the thermosphere, the stratosphere and the troposphere. The troposphere has four cloud layers: methane, hydrogen sulphide and ammonia, ammonium hydrosulphide, and water clouds at the upper limit. The stratosphere sits between the troposphere and the outermost layer, the thermosphere. Uranus’s pale blue color is due to the methane in its atmosphere.

Deeper inside the planet, it gets very hot. A deep, superheated layer of water, ammonia, and methane boils and bubbles up gases that form into clouds. Until Voyager 2 explored Uranus’s atmosphere, we didn’t know much about its features. The probe found a bright polar cap at the south pole, as well as a lighter band called a collar. There are many more clouds in the northern hemisphere, which are brighter and at a higher elevation than the ones in the southern hemisphere. The southern collar had nearly disappeared, while one in the north had grown. Uranus has a faint set of rings, which were discovered in 1977. Its thin and dark rings might be broken-up moons. The larger moons of Uranus are like small terrestrial planets of ice and rock. The odd moon Miranda has high cliffs and winding valleys.
